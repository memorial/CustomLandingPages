WHAT IS BREAST CANCER?

It is caused by uncontrolled proliferation of milk-forming cells that make up the milk channel in the breast tissue.
Breast cancer accounts for 33% of all cancers in women. It threatens 20% of all cancer patients.


Today, one out of every 8 women is at risk of facing breast cancer for the rest of her life.

The incidence of breast cancer increases with age. The risk of breast cancer, which is mostly seen in the 50-70 age group, increases when there is a family history of breast cancer. When breast cancer is seen in the mother or sibling, the risk of the disease increases 3-fold. For this reason, people with breast cancer in the family history should be checked frequently especially with the age of 40. Disruption of BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes, in other words, people with mutation are at high risk of developing both breast and ovarian cancer. Using hormone medication for more than 5 years in menopause is one of the factors that increase the risk of breast cancer.


What are the Symptoms of Breast Cancer?

Breast cancer symptoms can be listed as follows;


At nipples and their surroundings, color and shape change, nipple enlargement, flattening, inward collapse, deflection, crusting, cracks and wounds.

● Bloody or bloodless discharge from the nipple.

● Painful or painless swelling visible under the armpit.

● Just because you have a risk factor doesn't mean you're going to be infected.

Breast Cancer Risk Factors

People with less fat in the breast tissue increase the risk of breast cancer. The density of breast tissue can be measured by mamographic and sonographic methods. Radiotherapy is one of the risk factors for breast cancer because it is applied near the chest especially in lymphoma patients. Therefore, it is recommended that patients who receive radiotherapy, especially lymphoma patients, should have lifelong controls frequently after treatment.


DIAGNOSIS

Every woman should recognize her breast as of the age of 20, perform regular monthly checks and follow up mammography from the age of 40.

8 out of every 10 masses in the breast are benign; that is not cancer.


Early Diagnosis Methods in Breast Cancer

● Self breast controls

● Annual mammography

● Annual medical examination

Mammography should be performed at least once in the 30s.

It provides early diagnosis and complete elimination of the disease.


Breast Cancer Treatment Methods

Surgical Methods

The primary goal of breast cancer surgery is to remove the tumor so that it is not left behind and to remove the lymph nodes completely in cases that have spread under the armpit.

Mastectomy

Simple mastectomy: This procedure is also called total mastectomy. The entire breast, including the nipples, is removed, but no axillary lymph nodes or muscle tissue under the breast are removed. It is a method that is not preferred much today.

Skin protection mastectomy: In some female patients, the breast can be reconstructed during surgery. This procedure is called skin-protective mastectomy. Most of the skin on the breast (including areola and nipple) is left intact.

Lymph Gland Surgery

In order to determine the spread of breast cancer to the axillary lymph nodes, one or more lymph nodes are taken and examined under a microscope. If cancer cells are found in the lymph glands, the cancer has a high chance of spreading to other parts of the body through the bloodstream. The presence of cancer cells in the axillary lymph nodes plays an important role in deciding what kind of treatment will be applied if necessary.

Radiotherapy Treatment
In breast cancer, radiotherapy is aimed to destroy cancer cells that may remain in the armpit and breast area after surgery. In the treatment of breast cancer, radiotherapy is applied especially in patients with high risk of renewal and breast conserving surgery in order to protect the remaining tissue of the breast.
